Welcome to my website

I am a biotechnologist and I am working on DNA based data storage. I have done PhD in this field and I have published a few research journals and book chapters. I have also presented my work in some international conferences. Currently I am working at NUS as a postdoctoral researcher.

My research interests include DNA synthesis, sequencing, encoding, decoding, error correction, and compression. I am fascinated by the potential of DNA as a universal and durable medium for storing digital information. I am also interested in the applications of DNA data storage in biotechnology, medicine, and security.

Here you can find more information about me, my publications, my research projects, and how to contact me. I hope you enjoy browsing my website and learning more about my work.

About me

My name is John Smith and I am a biotechnologist. I have a PhD in DNA data storage from the University of Oxford, where I worked under the supervision of Professor Jane Doe. My thesis title was "Synthesis, sequencing, and encoding of DNA for digital information storage".

After completing my PhD, I joined the National University of Singapore (NUS) as a postdoctoral researcher in the Department of Biomedical Engineering. There, I am working on a project funded by the Singapore National Research Foundation (NRF) to develop novel methods for DNA data storage and retrieval. My project aims to improve the efficiency, accuracy, and scalability of DNA data storage systems.

I have published several research papers and book chapters in prestigious journals and conferences, such as Nature Biotechnology, IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, and ACM SIGMOD. You can find a list of my publications in the publications section of this website.

I have also received several awards and honors for my research, such as the Best Paper Award at the International Conference on Data Engineering (ICDE) 2022, the Young Investigator Award from the International Society for Computational Biology (ISCB) 2023, and the NRF Fellowship for Artificial Intelligence 2024.

Besides research, I enjoy teaching and mentoring students. I have taught courses on bioinformatics, data science, and machine learning at NUS and Oxford. I have also supervised several undergraduate and graduate students on their research projects.

My research projects

Here is a list of my current and past research projects related to DNA data storage. You can click on the links to access the project websites or reports.

  • DNA Data Storage and Retrieval using Nanopore Sequencing: This is my current project at NUS, funded by the NRF. The goal of this project is to develop a novel method for retrieving data from DNA using nanopore sequencing, which is a fast and portable technology that can read single molecules of DNA. The project involves designing and testing new encoding and decoding algorithms, as well as optimizing the synthesis and sequencing processes.
  • DNA Data Storage using Microfluidics: This was my previous project at NUS, in collaboration with the Singapore Institute of Manufacturing Technology (SIMTech). The aim of this project was to create a scalable system for DNA data storage using microfluidics, which is a technology that can manipulate small volumes of fluids on a chip. The project involved developing and testing new microfluidic devices, as well as integrating them with DNA synthesis and sequencing modules.
  • DNA Data Storage Techniques and Challenges: This was my PhD project at Oxford, supervised by Professor Jane Doe. The objective of this project was to survey the state-of-the-art techniques and challenges in DNA data storage, and to propose new solutions for improving the performance and reliability of DNA data storage systems. The project involved conducting a comprehensive literature review, as well as performing theoretical and experimental analysis.
  • DNA Data Storage using CRISPR-Cas9: This was my undergraduate project at Oxford, co-supervised by Professor Jane Doe and Professor John Roe. The purpose of this project was to explore the possibility of using CRISPR-Cas9, which is a gene editing tool that can insert or delete DNA sequences, for data storage purposes. The project involved designing and testing new CRISPR-Cas9 constructs, as well as evaluating their efficiency and accuracy.
